# How does valuable knowledge spread?

**Question.** What are the fundamental mechanisms by which humans evaluate which ideas to spread, and what (if anything) gives objectively *good* ideas an edge over bad ones?

**Short answer.** Idea-spread is governed by a layered selection system:

1. **Cognitive filters** inside each individual head (content biases, epistemic vigilance) — the first, weakest filter. They are sensitive to plausibility cues but also to features that have nothing to do with truth (novelty, emotional salience, simplicity).
2. **Social filters** in face-to-face interaction (model biases — copy the prestigious, the successful, the conformist majority; argumentative scrutiny of others' claims). Truth-correlated only as a side-effect of more general adaptive heuristics.
3. **Institutional filters** with skin-in-the-game (markets, prediction markets, peer-reviewed science, replication, courts, free press). The only mechanisms that systematically *couple* belief to ground truth, and even they do so imperfectly.

Truth gets an edge **mainly at long time scales and within institutions designed for it.** On short time scales and outside such institutions, truth has no built-in advantage; in fact, the largest empirical study to date — Vosoughi, Roy, & Aral (2018) on Twitter — found false news spread *farther, faster, deeper, and more broadly* than true news on every dimension measured (Vosoughi et al. 2018, *Science* 359:1146–1151). The mechanisms that eventually correct this are slow, institutional, and historically contingent.

Below: the mechanisms in detail.

---

## 1. Why naïve idea-spread is *not* truth-tracking

### 1.1 Content biases (Boyd & Richerson; Sperber)
People preferentially acquire ideas with certain content features regardless of truth (Henrich, Boyd & Richerson 2008). These include:

- **Sensory/visceral salience** — fatty/sweet foods, sexual content, gore, threat (innate biases shaped by ancestral fitness, not truth).
- **Minimally counter-intuitive concepts** — Boyer's explanation for the universality of religious concepts: a god is mostly like a person but with one or two violations (invisible, immortal). Memorable, transmissible, false.
- **Cognitive attractors** (Sperber 1996; Henrich, Boyd & Richerson 2008): inferential transformation during recall reshapes ideas toward easier-to-think variants. This is why folk biology converges on essentialism even when the underlying world is messier.

**Consequence.** A more truthful idea that is harder to think can lose to a less truthful idea that fits cognitive attractors better.

### 1.2 The novelty / emotion bias on social media
Vosoughi, Roy & Aral (2018, *Science*) analysed ~126,000 fact-checked rumour cascades on Twitter (2006–2017, ~3M users, 4.5M tweets):

- Falsehood diffused **significantly farther, faster, deeper, and more broadly** than truth in every category.
- Top 1% of false cascades reached 1,000–100,000 people; truth rarely exceeded 1,000.
- False stories elicited **fear, disgust, surprise**; true stories elicited anticipation, sadness, joy, trust. Falsehoods scored higher on a "novelty" measure.
- **Bots spread truth and falsehood at the same rate.** The asymmetry is human, not algorithmic.

This is the empirically strongest demonstration that, absent institutional correction, the spread of an idea is governed by *novelty × emotional intensity*, not truth.

### 1.3 Misinformation persistence
Lazer et al. (2018, "The science of fake news," *Science*) note three reinforcing factors: limited individual attention, motivated reasoning along partisan lines, and platform architectures that reward engagement (which correlates with novelty/emotion, not accuracy). Even after correction, the original false claim leaves a "continued influence effect" on belief.

## 2. Social-level filters: weakly truth-correlated by accident

Cultural-evolution theorists (Boyd & Richerson 1985, *Culture and the Evolutionary Process*; Henrich 2016, *The Secret of Our Success*) decompose social learning into two heuristic families: **content biases** (the *what*) and **context biases** (the *who* and *how many*). Context biases are the ones that do most of the practical work in human societies.

### 2.1 Prestige and success bias (Henrich & Gil-White 2001)
> "An individual will do better if they preferentially pay attention to, and learn from, people who are highly successful, particularly skilled, and/or well-respected" (Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan, *Dual Inheritance Theory* 2010, p. 562).

The heuristic *"copy whoever is observably succeeding"* is truth-correlated *to the extent that success is causally downstream of the idea being copied.* When the link from idea → success is direct (a better fishing technique catches more fish), this is a strong filter. When the link is mediated by many intervening variables (a CEO got rich for many reasons, only some of which are their stated principles), the heuristic becomes a vector for cargo-cult adoption of irrelevant traits — what Henrich calls "informational free-riding" on a noisy signal.

### 2.2 Conformist transmission (Boyd & Richerson 1985; Henrich & Boyd 1998)
> "Conformist transmission ... allows individuals to aggregate information over the behaviour of many individuals. Because these behaviours implicitly contain the effects of each individual's own experience and learning efforts, conformist transmission can be the best route to adaptation in information-poor environments" (Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan 2010).

When others' behaviour is itself the product of independent learning, copy-the-majority is a Bayesian-rational aggregator. This is why diverse groups can outperform smart individuals (see §3.1). But when others are themselves copying — an *information cascade* (Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer & Welch 1992) — conformity stops aggregating evidence and starts amplifying whichever variant got an early lead, regardless of truth.

### 2.3 Epistemic vigilance (Sperber et al. 2010)
A counterweight. Sperber, Clément, Heintz, Mascaro, Mercier, Origgi & Wilson (2010, *Mind & Language* 25:359–393) argue that humans evolved a *suite* of cognitive defenses against being misinformed:

- **Vigilance toward the source** (Is this person reliable? Are they competent? Do they have an interest in deceiving me?).
- **Vigilance toward the content** (Is this consistent with what I already know? Is the argument coherent?).
- **Reasoning** itself, in their account, is a tool of epistemic vigilance: it produces and evaluates arguments.

The crucial point is that vigilance is **biased toward incoming claims, not one's own.** This sets up the next mechanism.

### 2.4 The argumentative theory of reasoning (Mercier & Sperber 2011)
Mercier & Sperber (2011, *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 34:57–74) argue that human reasoning evolved primarily for **arguing in groups**, not for solitary truth-seeking. The empirical evidence:

- Individuals exhibit massive **myside bias** when generating their own reasons (poor truth-tracking).
- Individuals are **good at evaluating others' arguments**, especially when motivated to find flaws.
- In group discussion of *intellective tasks with demonstrably correct answers*, the group typically converges on the truth even when most members start wrong (the "truth wins" effect; Laughlin 1980).

**Mechanism.** Bad arguments fail under cross-examination; good arguments survive. The biases of producers and evaluators compose into a system that, *in groups with sufficient disagreement and free exchange*, produces population-level truth-tracking from individually biased reasoners. Take away the disagreement (echo chambers) and the system breaks: groups with shared priors radicalise rather than converge on truth (Sunstein 2002, *Law of Group Polarization*).

## 3. Institutional filters: the only systematic truth-tracking machinery

These are mechanisms designed (or selected) to couple beliefs to ground truth via feedback. The common feature is **costly skin-in-the-game**: an agent who promotes a wrong idea pays a cost they would not pay if they were right.

### 3.1 The wisdom of crowds and the Hong–Page diversity theorem
Surowiecki (2004) popularised; the math is in Hong & Page (2004, *PNAS* 101:16385–16389; though see Thompson 2014 critique and follow-ups). The condition under which a group beats its best individual member is **error independence**: members must err in *different* directions. Then averaging cancels error.

This works only if:
- Judgements are made independently (no information cascade).
- Members have diverse information / heuristics / models.
- Truth is one-dimensional and aggregable.

Condorcet's jury theorem (1785) is the formal ancestor: if voters are slightly better than chance and independent, the majority is almost certainly right as N → ∞. *Independence* is the load-bearing assumption — and in reality it is exactly what social dynamics tend to destroy.

### 3.2 Markets and prediction markets
Prices in competitive markets aggregate distributed information (Hayek 1945, "The Use of Knowledge in Society"). Wolfers & Zitzewitz (2004, *Journal of Economic Perspectives* 18:107–126) review the empirical record on *prediction markets*: contracts that pay out conditional on events. Their findings:

- Market prices typically outperform polls and expert panels in accuracy (e.g., Iowa Electronic Markets vs. polling for U.S. presidential elections).
- Information aggregates rapidly because participants have **financial incentive to act on private knowledge**.
- Wrong beliefs are punished by losses; correct beliefs are rewarded.

**This is the cleanest example of a truth-tracking institution.** The selection pressure on idea-holders is: *believe wrong things and lose money*. Robin Hanson (2007, "Insider Trading and Prediction Markets") has argued for "futarchy" — government by prediction markets — precisely because of this property.

The catch: prediction markets only track *empirical* truth on questions with a clear resolution mechanism. They cannot tell you whether an idea is *valuable* in a normative sense, only whether it is correctly describing a future state of the world.

### 3.3 Superforecasting (Tetlock)
Tetlock & Gardner (2015, *Superforecasting*) report on the IARPA Good Judgment Project, a multi-year geopolitical forecasting tournament. The top 2% of forecasters ("superforecasters") consistently beat both intelligence-community analysts (with classified information) and prediction-market prices. What distinguished them:

- Probabilistic, granular thinking (not "yes/no" but "67% likely").
- Aggressive belief-updating on small evidence.
- Active search for disconfirming evidence.
- "Foxes," not "hedgehogs" — many small models, not one big theory.

Implication: **a small disciplined subpopulation can dominate the truth-tracking accuracy of much larger groups, but only if their forecasts are aggregated by an institution that keeps score.** Without the scoring institution, the same individuals are indistinguishable from any pundit.

### 3.4 Science as a truth-tracking institution
Science is the most ambitious institutional attempt at truth-tracking. Its key mechanisms:

- **Falsification** (Popper 1934) — theories must make predictions that could fail.
- **Replication** — independent reproduction of results filters out chance, fraud, and idiosyncratic methodology.
- **Peer review** — gatekeeping by domain experts.
- **Citation networks** — Shwed & Bearman (2010, *American Sociological Review* 75:817–840) show empirically that scientific consensus on a question can be measured by the *decline in modularity* of citation networks: when contention falls, dissenting clusters disappear and the proposition becomes a "black box" treated as fact. They demonstrate this for the carcinogenicity of smoking, the safety of vaccines, and the non-effect of coffee on cancer.

But science is **not** a perfect truth-tracker:

- Ioannidis (2005, "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False," *PLoS Medicine*) — under realistic assumptions about prior probability, statistical power, and researcher degrees of freedom, **most published positive findings in many fields are wrong.**
- Open Science Collaboration (2015, *Science* 349:aac4716) — only **36 of 100** psychology studies replicated significantly; effect sizes were on average half the original.
- Camerer et al. (2018, *Nature Human Behaviour*) — replication rate of 13/21 social-science studies in *Nature/Science*.

What science *does* have is **a long-run error-correction process**. Wrong findings, if important, eventually get re-tested and discarded. Continental drift was rejected in 1912 and accepted in the 1960s. The bacterial cause of stomach ulcers (Marshall & Warren) was ridiculed for ~15 years before becoming consensus. Kuhn (1962) framed this as paradigm shifts; Lakatos (1970) more charitably as "research programmes" that progressively degenerate or progress. The institutional details vary; the core property is that **institutional science makes it possible — though slow and politically expensive — to be proven wrong.**

### 3.5 The free press, courts, and adversarial procedure
A fourth institutional family worth flagging: adversarial systems where two parties with opposed incentives both have access to evidence and a neutral audience. Common-law courts, investigative journalism with a competing outlet, peer-reviewed criticism with right-of-reply. The mechanism is the same as Mercier & Sperber's argumentative theory, scaled up by institutional design: *bad arguments lose because the other side has skin in the game and is watching.*

## 4. Synthesis: what gives good ideas an edge?

The user's question can now be answered cleanly. Good ideas get an edge over bad ones via four mechanisms, in increasing order of reliability:

| Mechanism | Truth-correlation | Time scale | Failure mode |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|---------------|
| Content biases (cognitive attractors) | Weak; truth is one of many features | Immediate | Sticky falsehoods (religion, conspiracies, viral lies) |
| Context biases (prestige, conformity) | Indirect — depends on success → truth link | Years to decades | Cargo cults, information cascades, fashion |
| Argumentative discussion in groups | Moderate, *iff* diversity is preserved | Hours to years | Group polarisation in echo chambers |
| Skin-in-the-game institutions (markets, prediction markets, replicating science, adversarial press) | Strong, *iff* the institution is functional | Years to centuries | Replication crises, regulatory capture, paradigm lock-in |

The fundamental insight: **truth is not free.** Idea-spread defaults to whatever is novel, emotionally salient, prestigious, or majority-held. Truth-tracking requires building institutions that *make wrong beliefs costly* — through money lost, predictions failed, replications disconfirming, or arguments demolished.

This explains both the optimistic and pessimistic stylised facts:

- **Why so many bad ideas exist.** Default human idea-spread is not truth-tracking. Religions, conspiracy theories, urban legends, pseudo-medicine, and racism propagate via mechanisms 1–2 above, and these mechanisms long pre-date any institutional correction.
- **Why progress nevertheless happens.** When truth-tracking institutions exist and are reasonably healthy, accumulated evidence eventually overwhelms the noise. Smoking does cause cancer, the Earth does orbit the Sun, vaccines do work, plate tectonics is real, leaded gasoline did poison children. In each case, decades of skin-in-the-game institutional work was required to dislodge a comfortable false belief.
- **Why this is fragile.** When truth-tracking institutions are *captured* (regulatory capture), *degraded* (replication crisis, peer review without accountability), or *bypassed* (social media's content-bias-maximising algorithms), the long-run truth gradient flattens. The system collapses back to mechanisms 1–2.

A practical heuristic for someone reasoning about whether a particular idea is likely to be correct, given that it is widespread: **ask which mechanism is responsible for its spread.** If the answer is "many people independently tested it and were paid to be right," update toward true. If the answer is "it has a great hook, prestigious people endorse it, and everyone you know holds it," update very little.
